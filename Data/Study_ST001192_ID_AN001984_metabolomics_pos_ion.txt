#PROJECT
PR:PROJECT_TITLE                 	A large library of gut bacterial isolates paired with longitudinal multiomics
PR:PROJECT_TITLE                 	data enables mechanistic microbiome studies
PR:PROJECT_TYPE                  	Metabolite profiling of human stool from a healthy cohort
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	Here, we present the Broad Institute-OpenBiome Microbiome Library (BIO-ML), a
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	comprehensive collection of 7,758 gut bacterial isolates with 3,632 paired
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	genome sequences, and densely sampled multi-omic time series from many
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	individual humans. Our longitudinal data reveal (1) that microbial species
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	maintain stable population sizes within and across humans, (2) that commonly
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	used ‘omic survey methods are more reliable when using averages over multiple
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	days of sampling, (3) that variation of gut metabolites within people over time
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	is driven by amino acid levels, while differences across people are driven by
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	differences in bile acids, and (4) that functional evolution and genomic
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	diversification can be used to infer eco-evolutionary dynamics and in vivo
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	selection pressures for strains within individual people. The BIO-ML is a unique
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	resource that will enable hypothesis-driven microbiome research and the rational
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	design of microbial therapeutics.

#STUDY
ST:STUDY_TITLE                   	A library of human gut bacterial isolates paired with longitudinal multiomics
ST:STUDY_TITLE                   	data enables mechanistic microbiome research
ST:STUDY_TYPE                    	Stool metabolite profiling
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	Fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) is used in the treatment of
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	microbiome-associated diseases such as Clostridium difficile infections. In
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	order to develop synthetic therapeutics and customized disease treatments we
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	will need to understand the bacterial communities in the stool samples used in
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	such treatments. For this purpose, a microbiome library was generated using
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	human stool obtained from healthy human FMT recruited by OpenBiome, a non-profit
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	organization that provides fecal microbiome therapeutics. In addition to
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	characterizing the bacterial populations and obtaining bacterial isolates from
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	FMT samples, we conducted metabolite profiling with the goal of: (1) generating
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	a library of metabolites in FMT samples, (2) Identifying metabolites associated
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	with defined bacterial populations, and (3) identifying microbial metabolites
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	with immunoregulatory functions. We conducted metabolite profiling on a subset
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	consisting of 180 stool samples from 84 donors using four nontargeted liquid
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	chromatography mass spectrometry (LC-MS) methods. Generated data were processed,
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	isotopes removed, and adducts and fragments clustered. The identity of known
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	metabolites was determined based on matching retention times of neat standards
ST:STUDY_SUMMARY                 	run in parallel with the study.
